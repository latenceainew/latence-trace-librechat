**1. High-risk classification.** TalentScout AI is a high-risk AI system. It
falls squarely within Annex III(4)(a), which lists AI systems intended for
the recruitment or selection of natural persons — in particular to "analyse
and filter job applications" and to "evaluate candidates" — as high-risk
under Article 6(2). The vendor's reliance on the Article 6(3) derogation
fails on the facts: scoring on a 0–100 fit scale and ranking the top 20
candidates is not a "narrow procedural task" within the meaning of Article
6(3)(a) and the AI Office FAQ. It materially influences which candidates a
human recruiter ever sees, so it does not satisfy Recital 53's
non-material-influence test.

**2. Two most relevant deployer obligations.** As a deployer of a high-risk
AI system we must (i) assign human oversight to a competent and authorised
natural person and ensure relevant, sufficiently representative input data
under Article 26, and (ii) carry out a fundamental rights impact assessment
before going live under Article 27 — provided we fall within Article 27's
scope (a public body, provider of public services, or deployer of an Annex
III(5)(b)/(c) system). For a private HR pilot that is not in those
categories the second obligation in the strict sense of Article 27 does not
apply; in that case the second-most-relevant obligation is monitoring the
system in operation and informing the provider of incidents under Article
26's monitoring duty linked to Article 72.

**3. If scoring and ranking were removed.** It would change the analysis but
**not** automatically clear the system. Article 6(3)(a), read with Recital
53 and the AI Office FAQ, allows extraction of structured fields without
scoring, ranking or shortlisting to be treated as a narrow procedural task —
"an AI system that transforms unstructured data into structured data". The
derogation only holds, however, if the system performs **no profiling of
natural persons**: under the final subparagraph of Article 6(3), profiling
within the meaning of Article 4(4) GDPR always reverts the system back to
high-risk. So the answer is "yes, it would qualify for the derogation under
Article 6(3)(a) **only** if we can certify that the field-extraction does
not constitute profiling of natural persons".
